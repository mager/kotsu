## Design Context

### Users
Kotsu is for Mager first, but it should work for anyone who wants to learn Japanese with a calmer, more visual approach. The ideal user is an early learner studying in short sessions on a phone, including travelers preparing for Japan, returning learners rebuilding momentum, and curious beginners who want to understand how characters are constructed instead of brute-forcing flashcards.

The main job to be done is simple: open the app quickly, see useful Japanese clearly, and make steady progress from kana to radicals to kanji to real words. The experience should support both immediate travel usefulness and the longer-term payoff of radical-based literacy.

### Brand Personality
Calm, focused, quietly electric.

Kotsu should feel disciplined and refined rather than gamified. It should carry a little Tokyo night energy through precise accent color and typography, but the core mood should stay steady, clear, and beginner-friendly. This is a study object, not a mascot app.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction is editorial study tool meets Tokyo transit signage meets refined language-learning object. The interface should feel mobile-first, fast to enter, and visually memorable without turning into a game dashboard.

References already present in the product DNA include Kana, Genki, and Kanshudo, but Kotsu should remain more distilled and more art-directed than any of them. It should explicitly avoid Duolingo-style streak pressure, reward loops, mascot clutter, noisy flashcard walls, and generic productivity UI.

### Design Principles
1. Teach from atoms to meaning. Start with sounds, shapes, and radicals, then show how they unlock kanji and vocabulary.
2. Make progress feel navigable. Learners should always understand where they are, what unlocks next, and how each lesson fits the larger course.
3. Optimize for short, repeatable sessions. Kotsu should be something a learner can open in ten seconds and use meaningfully on a phone.
4. Balance Japan-now utility with long-term literacy. Survival words and quick recognition matter, but the system should also build lasting intuition for character composition.
5. Keep the energy restrained. Use selective neon accents and strong Japanese typography to create identity, but never let the interface become noisy, gamey, or overdesigned.
