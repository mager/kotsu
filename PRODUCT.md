# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Kotsu is for Mager first, but it should work for anyone who wants to learn Japanese with a calmer, more visual approach. The learner may be brand new, returning after a gap, preparing for a Japan trip, or practicing in short sessions on a phone. They benefit from visual structure, repeated exposure, clear progression, and immediate signs of how kana, radicals, kanji, and words connect.

## Product Purpose

Kotsu teaches the knack of Japanese by starting with the smallest useful pieces and letting learners move from recognition into composition and reading. The core path should go from beginner sounds into radicals, then into kanji families, readings, and useful vocabulary. Branches can explore memory hooks, semantic families, and real-word connections, but the learner should still feel one coherent course instead of scattered reference material.

Kotsu should support two goals at once:

1. **Japan-now usefulness** — quick access to kana, survival words, and recognizable building blocks that help a learner feel more capable during travel and daily exposure.
2. **Long-game literacy** — a radical-first understanding of how kanji are assembled, so learning compounds instead of resetting every session.

Success means a learner can tell where they are, what unlocks next, what they have already learned, and why a given lesson matters beyond itself.

## Brand Personality

Calm, focused, quietly electric.

Kotsu should feel like refined study material with a little Tokyo night energy: serious enough for repetition, warm enough for beginners, and visually memorable without turning into a game dashboard.

## Anti-references

Avoid streak anxiety, mascot-heavy gamification, cluttered textbook tables, generic flashcard walls, dopamine-driven reward loops, and interfaces that make kanji feel like disconnected symbols. Avoid a pure dictionary experience where learners search but do not progress.

## Design Principles

Teach from atoms to meaning. Begin with sounds, shapes, and radicals before asking learners to memorize full kanji.

Show the whole route. The learner should always understand both their local lesson and their position in the larger course.

Branch without losing completion. Optional-feeling detours can exist, but the curriculum should make it clear that all branches are part of mastery.

Make recognition visual. Use composition, repeated radicals, and family grouping so learners learn by seeing patterns, not only reading definitions.

Optimize for short sessions. The app should be useful in under a minute, especially on a phone, without feeling disposable.

Balance travel utility with literacy. Include immediately useful words and cues for Japan mode, but do not sacrifice the deeper radical-first learning model.

Keep practice light but honest. Short sessions should feel approachable while still moving through real Japanese material.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use high-contrast text, visible focus states, non-color cues for progress, and responsive layouts that work on phones first. Motion should respect reduced-motion preferences. Beginners should not need prior kanji vocabulary to understand a lesson label, and learners should be able to make progress without relying on streak mechanics or constant notifications.
