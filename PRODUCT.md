# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Kotsu is for early Japanese learners who need a calm, daily study surface for characters, radicals, kanji, and simple vocabulary. The learner may be brand new, returning after a gap, or practicing in short sessions on a phone. They benefit from visual structure, repeated exposure, and clear signs of what each character is made from.

## Product Purpose

Kotsu teaches the knack of Japanese characters by starting with the smallest useful pieces and letting learners branch through an infinite-feeling curriculum tree. The core path should move from beginner recognition into radicals, then kanji composition, readings, and vocabulary. Branches can explore memory hooks, semantic families, stroke shape, or word examples, but the learner should still see that all content belongs to one complete course.

Success means a learner can tell where they are, what unlocks next, what they have already learned, and how radicals combine into kanji they can actually read.

## Brand Personality

Disciplined, vivid, encouraging. Kotsu should feel like sharp study material with a little Tokyo night energy: serious enough for repetition, warm enough for beginners, and visually memorable without turning into a game dashboard.

## Anti-references

Avoid streak anxiety, mascot-heavy gamification, cluttered textbook tables, generic flashcard walls, and interfaces that make kanji feel like disconnected symbols. Avoid a pure dictionary experience where learners search but do not progress.

## Design Principles

Teach from atoms to meaning. Begin with strokes, shapes, and radicals before asking learners to memorize full kanji.

Show the whole route. The learner should always understand both their local lesson and their position in the larger course.

Branch without losing completion. Optional-feeling detours can exist, but the curriculum should make it clear that all branches are part of mastery.

Make recognition visual. Use composition, repeated radicals, and family grouping so learners learn by seeing patterns, not only reading definitions.

Keep practice light but honest. Short sessions should feel approachable while still moving through real Japanese material.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use high-contrast text, visible focus states, non-color cues for progress, and responsive layouts that work on phones. Motion should respect reduced-motion preferences. Beginners should not need prior kanji vocabulary to understand a lesson label.
